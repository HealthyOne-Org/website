The research is clear: eating less can mean nutrient gaps

A 2026 review in the medical journal Clinical Obesity pooled six studies covering 480,825 adults taking GLP-1 medications. The pattern was consistent across all of them.

13.6%
developed a vitamin D deficiency within 12 months
54%

2.6%

480,825

Researchers concluded that doctors should treat nutrient deficiency as “a common consequence” of appetite-reducing therapy — and monitor patients' nutritional status accordingly. — Micronutrient and Nutritional Deficiencies Associated With GLP-1 Receptor Agonist Therapy, Clinical Obesity, 2026 (Urbina et al.); covered by Harvard Health and BBC Science Focus

Why it happens — and why you can't feel it

It's simple math. Eat roughly 30% less food and you take in roughly 30% fewer nutrients — whether the smaller appetite comes from a GLP-1 medication, a doctor-supervised program, a new eating plan, or just a busy stretch of life. The catch: a vitamin D, iron, or B12 gap has no obvious early symptoms. By the time the fatigue shows up, you've often been running short for months.
